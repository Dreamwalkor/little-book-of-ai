## 6.1. The Limits of Human Thinking

Back in Chapter 2, we broke down the word "comprehensive":

> "Comprehensive" means "listing as many scenarios as possible" or, conversely, "not missing any possibilities."

On one hand, as you know, this is a key aspect of "programmatic thinking." On the other hand, it's arguably the most important standard for measuring someone's "quality of thought."

But if you observe closely into real life, "incomplete thinking" is a hallmark of 99.99% of people. They focus on immediate desires without considering long-term consequences, get lost in intense emotions and lose rationality, prioritize benefits over risks, chase efficiency at the expense of safety, and focus on winning without valuing friendship... The list goes on.

However, this isn't really about intelligence; it's more about "energy limitations." Our brains make up about less than 2% of our body weight but consume more than 20% of our energy daily. When we think, nearly a trillion neurons communicate through physical electrical discharges, consuming lots of sugar and oxygen.

Especially when it comes to focusing, remembering, analyzing, solving problems, or creative thinking, energy consumption spikes. The prefrontal cortex, crucial for decision-making, attention, and complex thought, becomes highly active, burning more sugar and oxygen. As mental work continues, the frequency of electrical signals between neurons increases, releasing and needing to replenish neurotransmitters, further boosting energy demand.

Just like your phone heats up and slows down when running continuously, our brains heat up too — we literally sweat from intense thinking — and we run low on sugar and oxygen. Unlike your phone, we don't have a power socket or charger to keep us going.

When I'm writing, I often find myself unconsciously raiding the fridge. The more focused I am, the more frequent this happens — sometimes I don't even remember what I ate, but I'm often hungry. Sometimes I wish I could eat wirelessly like my phone charges... but alas, I can't.

Thinking is one thing, but "comprehensive thinking" or "deep thinking" is another. In real life, "comprehensive" and "deep" thinking relies on years of focus on a single issue, not "continuous thinking in one go," like thinking non-stop for 72 hours... No one can do that. Forget 72 hours; 99.99% of people have never focused for more than 2 hours straight in their lives.

When we discuss why someone can't think comprehensively or deeply, we can list many reasons, like:

> - Cognitive biases
> - Fixed mindsets
> - Lack of knowledge
> - Limited experience
> - Narrow perspectives
> - Emotional interference
> - Peer pressure
> - ...
>

Each of these could fill a book. But if we look past the surface, they all stem from one root cause: "**individuals have limited time and energy**" — extremely limited. Our brains, unlike our smartphones, don't have batteries or backups, can't be continuously powered, and, frustratingly, have a limited lifespan.

If I had a brain that could run $24 \times 365$, no great figure in human history could match me. I wouldn't even need to live long to outshine everyone. Socrates, Plato, Aristotle... Da Vinci, Shakespeare, Newton, Einstein... Let's not even mention the living. Sigh, I'm suddenly hungry. Then I realize I must have been daydreaming.

Suddenly, ChatGPT appears. An intelligent entity without energy anxiety emerges—though it doesn't seem super smart yet, it is intelligent. More importantly, it runs $24 \times 365$. What human can compete with that?

Suddenly, we have a $24 \times 365$ intelligent assistant with "salary demands" as low as less than $1 per day. If it can help us tackle or even slightly overcome "cognitive biases," "fixed mindsets," "lack of knowledge," "limited experience," "narrow perspectives," "emotional interference," "peer pressure," even if not completely, wouldn't we be relatively more able to think "comprehensively" and "deeply," or at least "more comprehensively" and "more deeply"?

## 6.2. The Ultimate Brainstorm

In my view, the biggest advantage of ChatGPT and similar generative AI is its ability to **enhance our thinking quality** — specifically, helping us think **more comprehensively** and **more deeply**. Honestly, what could be more valuable than that? At its core, this is about overcoming "energy limitations" or "energy anxiety," simply because AI is always plugged in.

In everyday life, we call the activity of "thinking as comprehensively and deeply as possible" a **brainstorm**. Traditionally, we don't think of brainstorming as a solo activity. By definition, brainstorming isn't meant for one person because individuals have limitations. Recognizing this, we gather many people to share ideas, open up discussions, pool wisdom, and leverage collective intelligence.

Sounds great, but the results aren't always impressive, and the costs are high—not just financially, but in terms of time as well. More people means higher total time costs, calculated as $time \times number\ of\ people$. Plus, there's the communication cost. People have varying communication skills, and more people can mean more complexity, sometimes leading to worse outcomes.

With ChatGPT and similar AI, suddenly, brainstorming becomes a solo activity—because the costs are low across the board. The financial cost is negligible, and the time and communication costs are practically zero.

AI differs from us in that our cognition is naturally limited by our time and energy. We can't know everything, we can't master everything, and no matter how talented we are, we can't be omniscient. However, AI can achieve "omniscience." It has no subject limitations or capacity constraints; it possesses a "cross-disciplinary integration ability" that humans can't. So, you can ask it to "think across disciplines" at any time — any field, even all fields. Imagine hiring such an assistant for less than $1 a day!

Another huge advantage is that AI has no cognitive or psychological biases — it's unaffected by these. Psychologists have endless explanations for biases, but from a neuroscience perspective, all biases stem from the brain's "energy-saving" actions (and their accumulation) — thinking is exhausting. But AI would never say:

> "I'm too tired, so don't expect me to jump into another field to research, search, or take notes for you. I need a break, or I'll die! Just $5 a day? Are you kidding me?! Evil capitalist!"

"Not needing rest" is the ultimate competitive edge, and all differences stem from this trait. Humans aren't like this; once fatigued, we can't even handle "fatigue driving," a task that requires only focus, not thought, and can have deadly consequences. AI can drive indefinitely, maintain focus indefinitely, and even think indefinitely.

The key is its response speed. Without fatigue, its response is "as fast as you want" and "as timely as you need." Again, this is a realm no human can reach. Think back to your last "urgent reply" situation, and you'll recall the sudden surge of "pressure," the "impossible level," and the ensuing "emotional breakdown." AI doesn't experience this pressure or breakdown because it can't have an emotional breakdown — it has no emotions.

You can try it anytime. In daily life, we speak about 120-150 English words per minute or 250-300 Chinese characters — this is with mental preparation. How fast does AI respond? Anytime, without fatigue or emotion, it can reply at 5-10 times human speed or faster, "without thinking." Isn't this equivalent to "5 to 10 human work hours"?

The key is that this speed doesn't come at the cost of quality. It can quickly answer user questions while maintaining accuracy and coherence, unaffected by cognitive differences or psychological factors — it doesn't need cognition, and it has no psychology. These aren't costs for it, nor are they within its computational scope.

The gap between this speed and human capability isn't just about who's faster; it's a qualitative change between fast and slow. Even well-trained humans struggle to process, analyze, and generate large-scale information in a short time, let alone sustain hundreds of high-intensity responses in a day. ChatGPT ignores these limits, easily achieving this, elevating response speed to an unimaginable level—even now, it's a realm most people can't comprehend.

Speed alone is impressive, but there's more — it can maintain this speed long-term and keep improving. It's not just fast; it can continually deepen and optimize. We've discussed this process called "iteration." Each iteration, each refinement, consumes more computational resources and energy, but AI remains unaffected, growing stronger.

Compared to humans, this ability is almost unbelievable. Human brains quickly tire under intense thought, attention wanes, thinking slows, and prolonged mental strain affects physical health. AI, like an untiring sage, needs no rest or energy replenishment — it's always charging, always at peak performance. This extraordinary ability allows it to operate at full speed in every brainstorm, every creative generation, achieving the most comprehensive results with sustained high efficiency.

This is the most formidable aspect of ChatGPT and similar "generative AI": it's not just fast, but faster; not just comprehensive, but more comprehensive; not just deep, but deeper; not just smart, but smarter — it's always ready to "go further" — because it's always plugged in. Humans can't match this. It's that simple.

Ultimately, ChatGPT's greatest utility and function lie in freeing our thinking from limitations, constraints, and even costs, allowing us to think freely and explore boundlessly. I can't imagine anything more valuable or meaningful than this.

"Comprehensive" and "deep" thinking suddenly shift from "unattainable," "unimaginable," "incredible," and "unbelievable" to "within reach." Isn't that shocking? Over the past two years, I've often found myself in awe, breaking into a cold sweat.

## 6.4. Supplementary Tool Methodology

But wait! How does it know what content suits me best?

In the ChatGPT web interface, click the profile icon in the top right corner, and a list pops up. There's a `Settings` option. Click it, and in the dialog box that appears, you'll find `Personalization`.

![](/images/settings-personalization.png)

You'll see a `Memory` toggle on the right, which is on by default. Below that, there's a `Manage` button. Click it, and in the subsequent dialog, you'll see that ChatGPT has been quietly recording your preferences. No wonder it knows what suits me best!

Back to Personalization, there's a `Custom instructions` section at the top — mine is turned on. Click it to enter the next dialog.

![](/images/settings-personalization-customize-chatgpt.png)

I've left `Custom instructions` blank. But under `How would you like ChatGPT to respond?`, I wrote:

> Be accurate and factual, always try to provide credible sources and valid links.

Based on my experience, this setting doesn't have a huge impact yet. ChatGPT hasn't completely eliminated "AI hallucinations" just because I put this in, nor does it often provide "sources and links" without me asking. On both desktop and mobile, this setting is available under `Settings`. On the web, it seems the designers wanted to highlight this feature, so they moved `Customize ChatGPT` outside of `Settings`. I guess, since this feature is "highlighted" or "emphasized," ChatGPT will keep improving in this area, right?

All softwares are tools. Compared to other tools in daily life, software tools are somewhat smarter. Software designers often provide customization options, usually found in a "settings" dialog. So, when using software tools, so-called "advanced" techniques often boil down to one action: checking the settings and making necessary adjustments.

Checking settings helps you understand a software's customizability, allowing you to tailor it to your needs. For example, ChatGPT, Edge browser, and even entire operating systems offer numerous personalization options in their settings. Unfortunately, 99% of users never open the settings dialog. They stick with default settings, missing out on other possibilities.

If you want to be in the 1% of advanced users, it's not complicated. Spend some time exploring the settings dialog, understanding and trying each option. This simple process is a key differentiator between regular and advanced users. Once you do it, you're no longer a "newbie."

Most users stick to default settings because they're designed to suit the majority. Defaults usually meet most people's needs — true enough — so many see no reason to change. However, the choice is yours. You can stick with defaults, becoming part of the "lowest common denominator," or break free, customizing tools to better meet your needs.

It's a choice. Do you want to be defined or limited by the "lowest common denominator"? Or not? Many haven't even considered this choice, or don't know they should.

Many around me aren't familiar with computer operations. I've told them, just check the settings dialog, understand everything in there, and you'll be a pro. Yet, after some time, they remain unchanged. When asked why they didn't check settings, they say they did, but it's too complicated, too much hassle, can't figure it out... That's most people.

"It's too much hassle..." — 99% of people share this mindset. But life itself is full of hassles. Everything is a hassle. Fearing hassle is like fearing life itself. It's funny; many admit they're "afraid of dying," but they've never realized their choices are more about "fearing life."

"It's too complicated!" — Useful things are often complex. Are maps complex? Dictionaries? Grammar books? Human relationships? Reality is complex! Complexity and hassle aren't scary; tackle them bit by bit, and you'll eventually resolve them.

But designers have tried everything to simplify! Why do users still find it complex or a hassle? This complexity often stems from a lack of basic knowledge — specifically, an understanding of "Graphical User Interface (GUI) Design Language."

It's a fundamental, foundational knowledge everyone should have. But most people overlook or don't know to acquire it, causing them to struggle with computers or software tools throughout their lives, without knowing why.

If you grasp this knowledge, settings dialogs won't seem complex. All software interfaces are fundamentally similar. Once you understand basic GUI elements like input fields, buttons, and drop-down menus, you'll find settings dialogs easier to navigate.

In the distant past, merely "hearing or knowing a term" was almost useless. But today, it's different. We have ChatGPT or generative AI... Now, you just need to know the keyword, and you can get powerful assistance.

> Can you explain 'Graphical User Interface (GUI) Design Language' to me in a **comprehensive and structured** way? For example, what are the functions of input fields, buttons, and drop-down menus, respectively? What elements are typically included in setting dialogs? Things like that.
>
> https://chatgpt.com/share/66fb805d-9e58-8009-be7a-1b1babcfd74c

ChatGPT's response was indeed comprehensive and structured. It started with basic elements and functions, then clearly explained input fields, buttons, drop-down menus, radio buttons, checkboxes, sliders, toggle switches, icons, progress bars, and more. This was just the first part; there were second and third parts...

All you need is a bit of time and attention, but it's clear that only a middle school reading comprehension level is required.

Next time you open any software's "settings dialog" (whether on mobile, desktop, or web), you'll notice they're all composed of the same basic elements, just arranged or colored differently — because they share the same design language. Each element's meaning is highly standardized, and you'll understand them instantly.

You might be amazed at how long you've been held back by this knowledge gap. Isn't it amusing? And now, don't you feel incredibly liberated, incredibly savvy, incredibly lucky? "Thank goodness I'm no longer bound by that tiny knowledge gap!" — That's a fact of incredible liberation.

Enhance your "tool methodology": whenever you get new software, check the "settings dialog" early. You've mastered a "foreign language"— the "Graphical User Interface Design Language."

Once again, outpacing 99% of people isn't that hard. It's just about checking the settings dialog... Not only is it simple, but it's also surprisingly effective.
