# Foreword

About two hundred years ago, our world went through a seismic shift: the Industrial Revolution.

One "side effect" of this revolution was a general decline in human physical strength. Suddenly, people didn't need to engage in the kind of back-breaking labor that was once the norm. The result? A noticeable and undeniable drop in physical prowess for most folks. Fast forward a few generations, and if we could hop into a time machine back to those days, we'd be the weaklings of the past — the ones who couldn't even wring a chicken's neck.

By the 1960s, thanks to technological advances, abundant resources, improved healthcare, and better nutrition, another trend emerged. People started living longer. Life expectancy jumped from under 30 years in the late 19th century to nearly 80 by the early 21st century. But longer life didn't necessarily mean healthier life. Compare a typical beach photo from 1960 with one from 2023:

![](/images/1960-2023-beach.png)

On one end of the spectrum, most people's physical strength has taken a nosedive. On the other? There's a group of folks who've gained superhuman strength through "scientific training."

As modern humans, if you can crank out 10 push-ups in one go, you're probably in the top 20%. Sure, ancient folks might've had better overall stamina, but I doubt many could do 100 push-ups straight. Guess how many the world record holder can do now? In 1980, Japan's Minoru Yoshida did 10,507 push-ups non-stop. In 1993, Charles Servizio from the U.S. did 46,001 push-ups in 21 hours and 21 minutes.

It's not just about breaking "physical limits." More importantly, even though the percentage of people training is low, the absolute number of those with strong physical capabilities is on the rise.

In 1908, the Olympic marathon record was 2 hours, 55 minutes, and 18 seconds. Fast forward over a century, and the new record is about 2 hours, 2 minutes, and 57 seconds — shaving off over 50 minutes, a 30% reduction. A hundred years ago, only one person could run a marathon in under 2 hours and 55 minutes. Now? Around 30,000 runners qualify for the Boston Marathon each year, all beating the 3-hour, 5-minute mark.

This is how the once "earth-shattering" Industrial Revolution impacted human "physical strength":

> * Most people's physical strength has declined (that's a fact).
> * The percentage of people who exercise regularly has dropped (say, from 20% to 2%).
> * But among those who do exercise, a larger proportion achieve higher results than before (the success rate has increased, say, from 20% to 80%).
> * The best performances among exercisers are now unimaginable in the past (like 46,000 push-ups).
> * The gap between the first and second place keeps widening (for example, the second might only manage 5,000 push-ups).

Now, let's use this as a reference to imagine what the "sudden emergence" and "rapid development" of artificial intelligence might do to human "brainpower."

Just as the Industrial Revolution reduced the physical demands for survival, the mental demands for survival have been decreasing too. This isn't unique to the AI era; it's been a trend for years. One reason for the booming consumer society over the past 30-50 years is the widespread adoption of the "idiot-proof design principle" — products that don't require learning or thinking to use are the ones with the potential to sell big.

Now, this trend is about to be amplified by AI — because AI lowers the "difficulty of all learning." In other words, even if you have to learn or think, AI drastically reduces the cost of doing so. The evolution from "personal computers" to "mobile computers" to "smart mobile devices" is a prime example. Devices are getting lighter, smarter...

We can't say people are getting dumber, but it's true that "devices are getting simpler, easier to use, and more intuitive." Computers once had a steep learning curve. Now? While truly mastering them is getting harder, for most people, "idiot-proof smartphones" are not only sufficient but feel increasingly user-friendly.

So, the "current AI revolution's impact on human brainpower" might parallel or align with the "Industrial Revolution's impact on human physical strength":

> * Most people's brainpower is declining.
> * The percentage of people who engage in self-directed learning is dropping.
> * Among those who do learn, a larger proportion achieve higher results than before.
> * The best performances among learners are now unimaginable in the past.
> * The gap between the first and second place keeps widening.

In fact, this trend was already in motion before AI "suddenly appeared" — human brainpower has been on the decline, with the weak getting weaker and the strong getting stronger.

In 2015, India's Rajveer Meena became the recognized record holder by reciting the first 70,000 digits of π, taking 9 hours and 7 minutes. Japan's Akira Haraguchi claims to have recited an even more incredible 100,000 digits, nearly 200 times what anyone could do 42 years ago. Another example: India's Vikas Sharma can calculate the roots of 12 large numbers, each with 20 to 51 digits, in under a minute, faster than many can type the numbers into a calculator and read the answer.

"Remembering the first 70,000 digits of π" or "quickly calculating the 50th root of a 51-digit number" are mind-blowing examples of "super brainpower," much like "46,001 push-ups" show physical limits being shattered. But these examples often feel distant to most "ordinary folks," as if they have nothing to do with them.

Let's look at some more relatable facts. In the 1990s, scoring over 600 on the 677-point TOEFL exam in mainland China was rare, with most people struggling to hit 550. Now, with a new TOEFL scoring system out of 120, scores of 116 and above are common (equivalent to about 657 in the old system). Not only that, but the proportion of middle schoolers achieving high scores keeps rising.

The weak get weaker, the strong get stronger.

The trend is there.

What's left is the individual's choice within that trend...

— What's your choice?
