## 2.1. The Often Overlooked Rule: Safety First

"Safety always comes first." Sure, it sounds like a no-brainer, so much so that reminding folks might seem almost offensive. But the stats tell a different story.

Take ladders, for instance. They're about as basic as tools get, right? Yet, this straightforward, handy tool leads to over 130,000 emergency room visits in the U.S. every year. Why? Because people skip the "Safety First" rule. Most of these accidents happen due to obvious mistakes, like not securing the ladder properly or using it on a slippery surface. And let's not forget, around 300 ladder-related deaths occur annually. ([The Numbers Don’t Lie: Make Ladder Safety a Priority](https://blog.ansi.org/ali/make-ladder-safety-a-priority-injury-statistics/))

[Stats](https://www.safetymanualosha.com/hand-tool-safety/) show that roughly 8% of workplace injuries are linked to hand tools. In 2021, the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) reported over 90,000 cases of injuries requiring multiple days off due to improper hand tool use. About 95% of these were from misuse, with screwdrivers being one of the top culprits. People often hold screwdrivers with the tip facing up or use them for the wrong tasks, like prying or chiseling, leading to punctures, cuts, and eye injuries.

Next time you have a moment, ask folks around you, "Has anyone ever told you not to hold a screwdriver with the tip facing up?" Chances are, most will say no. This means a lot of people don't really take the "Safety First" rule seriously.

There's a great read on this topic:

> [Hand Tool Safety Aaron Reeves, Loss Control Representative/Biosystems Engineer, AMIC/MWC](https://almonline.org/Assets/Files/LossControl/ReferenceDocuments/Hand%20Tool%20Safety.pdf)

### 2.2.1. A Thought Process

Creating a table like this is pretty straightforward:

> * List comprehensively
> * Categorize list items
> * Iterate categorization

First, brainstorm as many factors as possible — ideally, all of them. You can ask ChatGPT for input, but also rely on your own thinking. Sift through ChatGPT's responses, add your own insights, and you'll end up with a more complete list.

Next, categorize the list. The most common method is the "binary split," dividing everything into two categories. For instance, "Risks/issues OpenAI can solve in the long run" versus "Risks/issues OpenAI might not solve anytime soon" — simplified to "OpenAI Can Fix" and "OpenAI Can't Fix."

The last step is both crucial and fun: taking a list and splitting it from different angles. "Iterate" means applying the same method again, but from a new perspective. So, I used "I Can Fix" and "I Can't Fix" for another split. The result? The table you see above.

This "iterative binary split" is a super handy analytical tool. If you read often, especially non-fiction, you'll notice this approach is not just useful but also quite common.

Take, for example, the "double-blind test" in medicine, which is essentially an "iterative binary split."

| Drug/Placebo         | Doctor Knows | Doctor Doesn't Know |
| -------------------- | ------------ | ------------------- |
| Patient Knows        |              |                     |
| Patient Doesn't Know |              | * Double-blind Test |

> Only when neither the doctor nor the patient knows if a placebo is being administered can the true effect of a drug be tested.

Even when joking around, the "iterative binary split" is delightfully clever:

| People   | Smart | Foolish |
| -------- | ----- | ------- |
| Diligent |       |         |
| Lazy     |       |         |

Then comes the thoughts:

> * Smart and diligent folks make great middle managers.
> * Smart and lazy ones are suited for leadership roles.
> * Foolish and lazy individuals are best avoided or let go.
> * Foolish and diligent ones are the most dangerous — they might cause unforeseen disasters.

This method is also great for explaining things to kids:

| Activity | Fun  | Boring |
| -------- | ---- | ------ |
| Useful   |      |        |
| Useless  |      |        |

Then comes the thoughts:

> Activities can be fun or boring, and useful or useless. We love doing things that are both fun and useful, but they're rare. We naturally avoid what's both boring and useless. Often, we must choose between "fun but useless" and "useful but boring." The answer is clear: in the long run, doing "useful but boring" things pays off more than indulging in "fun but useless" activities. This is a solid basis for decision-making.

Iterating the binary split twice gives you four scenarios. Do it three times, and you get eight scenarios. But visualizing that might require a 3D model, which is tricky to show on paper.

![3D Cube](/images/cube-2x2x2.svg)

Back to the topic, some risks or limitations, like AI misuse or algorithmic bias from training data, are beyond both OpenAI's and my control. Therefore, we just have to live with them.

For issues OpenAI can solve, whether I can fix them or not doesn't matter much. Over time, these limitations or risks will be addressed, like "outdated training data." OpenAI has already improved its "once poor web search capabilities" in newer versions. Similarly, "lack of logical reasoning" saw improvements with the release of GPT-o1-preview in September 2024. As for "insufficient expertise," especially in legal, medical, and financial fields, AI will likely advance quickly. As users, we can't rush it.

The real game-changer is "electricity cost." The biggest expense in AI is electricity, a continuous cost, unlike one-time hardware investments like GPUs. As electricity costs drop, AI will become smarter. This trend is irreversible, and commercial solar power is just around the corner.

The key is identifying which "OpenAI can't solve" or "short-term unsolvable" issues I can tackle now. Since I'm the one using it, if I can address some parts, I'll have a better experience. That's the focus, and that's where I should invest my time and energy.

### 2.2.2. The AI Hallucination

Ever since ChatGPT took the world by storm, another term has been making waves: "AI hallucinations." This concept dives into how "generative AI based on large language models" works. In simple terms, the "responses" you see from AI are calculated one word at a time, not "thought out" like a human would. The "generative" part of generative AI is all about this process:

> **1. Finding the Most Probable Word**
>
> When you ask AI a question, it doesn't truly understand your query. Instead, it calculates which word is most likely to fit the question based on its massive training dataset. This word is chosen by analyzing patterns in historical data and considering the context of your question.
>
> **2. Finding the Next Word**
>
> Next, it uses the first word to calculate the next most probable word. Again, the AI isn't "thinking" about what to write next like a human would. It's using statistical data to determine which word fits best.
>
> **3. Continuously Generating Sentences**
>
> This process repeats until it generates a complete sentence or paragraph. Each word is calculated and selected based on the previous ones, ensuring the generated content appears coherent and natural, like a full-fledged answer.
>
> **4. Organizing Natural Language**
>
> Thanks to extensive training data, generative models learn the basic rules and structures of human language. So, the sentences they generate usually align with linguistic norms. But this doesn't mean they understand the language; it's all about probabilistic calculations to produce the most "reasonable" output.

In a nutshell, AI doesn't think like humans — now or ever. It calculates patterns in data to probabilistically find the most fitting words, generating responses one word at a time. It's a mathematical process, not genuine "thinking."

Because one of the design principles and goals of generative AI is to "align with natural language habits," its responses, though calculated, feel natural to users. They might even come across as "decisive." But in reality, "plausible" is the more accurate term to describe them.

The issue is that AI can often sound "convincingly authoritative", yet merely "plausible", so we need to tread carefully.

Here's an example:

### 2.3.1. Option One: Fact-Checker (Chat Version)

The simplest approach? Start a new chat and tell ChatGPT:

> From now on, you're a diligent and strict fact-checker. Whenever I provide any information, scrutinize each factual statement. Give detailed feedback on which statements are accurate, which aren't, and explain why. If there's anything uncertain or unverifiable, point it out and suggest how to further verify it.

Whenever you need a "fact-check" in any other chat, just hop over to the "fact-check chat" and start with: "Do a quick fact-check this for me: ..." Then paste the text you need checked. This pre-set "fact-checker" will give you a thorough response.

### 2.3.2. The Importance of "Role Definition"

Let's keep the ball rolling.

By now, we've touched on a pretty advanced ChatGPT technique: "Role Definition."

So, what exactly is "Role Definition"? It's really just a strategy we use in everyday life. We often define roles for others, and then they have to respond according to that role. Take this line, for example:

> "You're a teacher, and this is how you talk?!"

That's an angry parent questioning a disrespectful teacher. But this line is actually a classic "role definition prompt." Once the teacher heard this, they have to consider their role in all future interactions, right?

I even have my own go-to role definition prompt for complaints. We all have those moments when we need to complain, don't we? Like when a ticket platform really ticks me off... I'm fuming... But I have a "complaint role definition prompt":

> [In a calm, emotion-free voice]
>
> I might accidentally raise my voice or even curse because I'm really upset. But please remember, my emotions aren't directed at you personally; they're aimed at this poor product, service, or even the company. You're just an employee, doing a tough, stressful job handling complaints, so I totally get your pressure. But at the same time, please understand me... We don't pay to get frustrated, right? Okay, I'm going to start now, and I'll try to keep my cool...

I probably use this once or twice a year when I have to complain. My complaints usually go smoothly and get resolved in one go — thanks to this "complaint role definition prompt."

In any ChatGPT client (whichever one you're using), each chat is like a seperated "clone" of ChatGPT. The first thing you say to this "clone" is understood as a "role definition" to some extent. If you clearly define it, it'll adjust better and more accurately according to this role.

So, when you start a new chat and give ChatGPT's "new clone" a clear "role definition," this clone becomes your "fact-checker bot."

However, given the current setup, ChatGPT can sometimes be "too smart" and adjust its "identity" on the fly. So, when talking to this defined clone, it's best to use a prefix like:

> Please help me fact-check:
>
> [The information you need checked]

If you forget to add this prefix and the clone's "role definition" changes unexpectedly (you might see a "Memory updated" message), you might need to remind it:

> From now on, you're a diligent and strict fact-checker. Whenever I provide any information, scrutinize each factual statement. Give detailed feedback on which statements are accurate, which aren't, and explain why. If there's anything uncertain or unverifiable, point it out and suggest how to further verify it.

## 2.4. Other Risks

In reality, for individuals, the risks posed by AI go way beyond just "AI hallucinations." There are plenty more. To get a better grasp, you might as well ask ChatGPT:

> Provide a detailed explanation of the potential risks of AI abuse for ordinary people.
>
> https://chatgpt.com/share/670f9e84-d8b4-8009-a65c-167d7f978df0

Besides what ChatGPT tells us about "privacy invasion and data misuse," "spread of misinformation," "job market disruption," "discrimination and bias," "social and psychological manipulation," "cybersecurity risks," "mental health issues," and so on, I want to discuss the increasingly severe issue of "information bubbles."

AI-generated content has become incredibly realistic, surpassing the point of "hard to distinguish from reality," whether it's text, audio, or video.

While the general public hasn't fully caught on, many podcasts are already using AI-generated voices. Even many radio stations, often seen as "stuck in the past," have over half of their broadcasts produced by "automated AI-generated audio content" — listeners just aren't aware.

Compared to humans, "machine-generated content" is produced at lightning speed and at a fraction of the cost. In fact, the proportion of content created by humans on Earth has long fallen below 1%—not just recently, but for quite some time. Many people struggle to accept these figures simply because they don't realize the sheer volume, reach, and popularity of what they consider "junk content."

Due to the efficiency gap between robots and real humans, the proportion of "human-created" content will continue to plummet. The prefix "*yacto*-" represents $\frac{1}{10^{24}}$... So, yactopercent would be $\frac{1}{10^{26}}$. What I'm saying is that in the foreseeable future, the proportion of "human-created" and high-quality content will drop to yactopercent...

This prediction isn't exaggerated.

## 2.6. Strategy and Quality

Looking back at the Fact Checker bot ([Fact Checker](https://chatgpt.com/g/g-IHm2zCAgi-fact-checker)), from a design quality perspective, it should theoretically be the best out there. But that's not because I'm particularly brilliant; it's because I asked ChatGPT to "*use your best design principles to draft a comprehensive and structured GPT role definition.*" So, it's not that other GPT designers can't compete with me, but rather, they can't really compete with ChatGPT... right?

This isn't the first time I've used this approach. In the investment world, I've applied the same logic. I spent a lot of time and effort researching various investment strategies and ultimately chose the "simplest yet most effective investment strategy" — the "regular investing strategy" (see "[On Regular Investing](https://github.com/xiaolai/regular-investing-in-box/tree/master/docs/en)"). Essentially, "regular investing" is just the most straightforward and feasible way to "follow the market." In the end, it's not that others can't outperform me, but rather, "no one can beat the market," plain and simple. I remain unchanged, not automatically improving because of this strategy, yet my investment results are excellent, and it's tough for anyone to beat them.

So, "strategy" really matters. Often, "effective strategies" aren't complicated; they're incredibly simple. The unfortunate truth is that most people don't value them, especially when they're simple. Ironically, "effective strategies" are often the most important when they're simple, and vice versa—the most important ones are usually the simplest.
