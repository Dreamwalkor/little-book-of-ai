# 10. Everyone Can Use English

![](/images/dalle/10.png)

It's a bummer, but English isn't a "middleware" skill. It's never been and never will be. In the long run, it should be a "terminal skill" for everyone. And its best use case might surprise you — it's not just about chatting with people anymore. It's about having meaningful conversations with AI, whose "native language" is English. Lucky or not, that's our second language.

## 10.1. Everyone Has Multilingual Potential

Back in the day, mastering a foreign language was a lifelong challenge for most people. But suddenly, it's not just about one language anymore. You could potentially master several — if you're willing to put in the work. While many think, "AI translation is so good now, why bother learning a language?" this is actually the era where everyone can use English, and even become a polyglot.

There have always been "bilingual families" on Earth, though they're a small slice of the population. For instance, parents might be from different ethnicities or countries, speaking different languages. Their kids grow up in a multilingual environment. I'm Korean-Chinese, and back home in Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture, some folks only speak Chinese, some only Korean, but many speak both. Even some pets can understand multiple languages.

Imagine seeing a dog on the street. You say "come here" in Chinese, and it ignores you. Maybe it's from a monolingual Korean family. But say "오라오라" and it comes running. Flip it around, and some dogs won't get "오라오라" but will respond to "来来来" — a sign they're from a monolingual Chinese family. Of course, there are plenty of dogs that understand both, making them bilingual pups.

There are also multilingual regions on Earth. Take Hunchun and Tumen in Yanbian, where the borders of China, North Korea, and Russia meet. Many people there grow up speaking three languages. Or look at Hong Kong, where folks often speak Mandarin, Cantonese, and English. Switzerland is another example, nestled between France, Italy, Germany, and Austria. People there might use three, four, or even five languages, including English, French, Italian, German, and Romansh. Swiss dogs might not get "来来来" or "오라오라," but they probably understand a few other languages like English, French, Italian, German, or Romansh.

Kids growing up in bilingual or multilingual environments naturally pick up and use multiple languages without any accent interference. The key is, they don't feel like they're putting in extra effort. It all feels natural, like it's just the way things are supposed to be. This isn't just true for people — theoretically, it's the same for those dogs.

This all shows that everyone has the potential to become a multilingual user.

## 10.2. The Unexpected Perks of Being Multilingual

Being a multilingual user comes with a ton of perks, and even ChatGPT can back me up on this — go ahead, ask it yourself:

> Elaborate the benefits of being a multilingual user in a comprehensive and structured way.
>
> https://chatgpt.com/share/6718872a-2360-8009-bd5b-22db4bcabcc5

But there are also some benefits that monolingual folks might not even think about. For instance, multilingual people tend to be more friendly. The more languages they know, the friendlier they seem. It might sound like there's no direct link, but if you think about it, it makes sense.

For starters, multilingual folks have a more diverse way of thinking. The more languages they know, the richer their perspectives. Plus, they spend a lot of time learning and practicing languages. Since time and energy are limited, they end up spending less time judging others. Also, all that learning helps them recognize their own shortcomings, which fosters humility and patience. Exposure to different cultures helps them break down stereotypes and biases. As they dive deeper into languages, their communication skills get a boost. All these factors combined make multilingual users some of the friendliest people around.

On top of that, multilingual folks have brains that are generally healthier. The most effective way to learn languages — no matter who you are or where you're from — is through "lots of reading aloud" and "frequent speaking."

Speaking is a super complex brain activity. Each sound requires the brain to coordinate around 100 muscles. Each muscle needs at least one "functional local network" of thousands of neurons to get the job done. Imagine the workout your brain gets from reading aloud or speaking for a while.

David Snowdon from the University of Minnesota conducted a long-term study starting in 1986, tracking the health and cognitive changes of nearly 700 nuns.

Even though some nuns lived past 100 and their brains showed damage similar to Alzheimer's patients, they didn't show cognitive symptoms while alive. A big reason was their habit of reading scriptures aloud, which acted like "aerobic exercise" for the brain, maintaining high "neural plasticity." Even if part of the brain was damaged, other healthy parts learned to take over, so they didn't show signs of cognitive decline.

> Snowdon, D. (2001). *Aging with Grace: What the Nun Study Teaches Us About Leading Longer, Healthier, and More Meaningful Lives*. Bantam Books.

Later studies have shown that multilingual people have a lower risk of dementia compared to monolinguals. For example, a 2013 study in *Neurology* found that multilinguals developed dementia about 4-5 years later than monolinguals. This study, conducted in India with 648 participants, showed that regardless of socioeconomic status, gender, or education, multilinguals had a lower chance of developing dementia in old age.

Cognitive reserve is thought to be a key factor in preventing brain diseases. A 2014 study in *PNAS* found that multilinguals maintain stronger brain connectivity in old age. This means they can keep higher cognitive function, even if their brains face similar pathological changes as monolinguals.

> Alladi, S., Bak, T. H., Duggirala, V., Surampudi, B., Shailaja, M., Shukla, A. K., & Kaul, S. (2013). *Bilingualism delays age at onset of dementia, independent of education and immigration status*. Neurology, 81(22), 1938-1944.
>
> Gold, B. T., Kim, C., Johnson, N. F., Kryscio, R. J., & Smith, C. D. (2014). *Lifelong bilingualism maintains neural efficiency for cognitive control in aging*. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 110(15), 7877-7881.

## 10.3. English at Your Fingertips

Suddenly, we're living in the age of AI. With tools like ChatGPT, we can now "use English directly" without breaking a sweat. AI has become our ultimate language teacher — and not just for English, but for any foreign language. No human teacher can really compete with that.

When it comes to using a foreign language, we usually find ourselves in two scenarios:

> * Real-time processing
>   * Listening
>   * Speaking
> * Asynchronous processing
>   * Reading
>   * Writing

### 10.3.1. Asynchronous Processing Scenarios

Let's dive into the "asynchronous processing" situation first. The "quick fix" we mentioned earlier for those who "can't use English" mainly targets the asynchronous side of things. Later, we'll talk about a "long-term solution" for mastering real-time English processing — something people have struggled with for over a decade.

The key scenarios for asynchronous foreign language processing are "reading" and "writing."

> * Even after checking all the words and phrases, if the sentence still doesn't make sense, you might need to consult a grammar book for unfamiliar structures.

## 10.4. Focus of Practice

>

### 10.4.1. Intensity

Let's talk about why "intensity" is crucial. Learning is about creating, and practicing is about learning well enough to create effectively and efficiently. At its core, no matter what you're learning, all practice is brain training. It's like the old saying, "Practice makes perfect." From a neuroscience perspective, it's about building new connections between neurons through lots of repetition, forming new networks, and solidifying those connections through repetition. That's the whole game.

So, you can't skimp on repetition. You could practice for 3 hours a day and rack up 1000 hours in a year. Or, you could practice for just 5 minutes a day and take 32.87 years to hit 1000 hours. The latter isn't just unrealistic; it's also ineffective. Building new connections is tough, and without enough repetition, those new connections can easily break or get overridden by existing ones.

In other words, "effort" is really just "enough repetition in a short period." It's practical — if you're willing to focus your attention for 1000 hours, you can theoretically master any skill you need. So, either don't practice at all, or if you do, practice intensively. There's no other way that works.

### 10.4.2. Systematic Approach

Let's dive into why being "systematic" is so crucial.

Think about all those years of "effort" and "struggle" — six years in elementary school, three in middle school, three in high school, four in college. Why do so many people spend so much time and energy only to end up with nothing to show for it? Take English, for example. Despite years of study, many can't speak fluently, read with ease, or even understand spoken English, let alone write. The root cause, besides not practicing enough or intensely, lies in a sneaky trap:

> **Knowledge was never systematized.**

Learning a natural language is more like following a spiral path. You start at a point and expand outward, always staying "systematic enough" within your current scope. In other words, your knowledge should always be a "minimum viable system," constantly expanding.

![spiral-lineage](/images/spiral.png)

For instance, when kids first learn to describe size, they might only know two words: *big* and *small*. It's basic, but it works for them. They likely learn these two words almost simultaneously because knowing just one isn't systematic or sufficient. Over time, they'll add words like *large* and *tiny*, and much later, they'll learn *massive*, *gigantic*, *teeny*, *minute*, *miniature*, *microscopic*, and so on.

Vocabulary needs to cover all aspects — size, shape, color, material, action, speed, time, direction. It's endless. Each aspect expands gradually, and progress across different areas should be somewhat parallel. It's unlikely someone can only use *big* and *small* for size but can describe shapes with words like *concave*, *crescent*, *pyramidal*, or *rhomboid*.

The same goes for native language phonetics, which are mostly set by age six and serve as the foundation for lifelong language use. Grammar takes longer to learn systematically. As for vocabulary, most people only master a fraction of it in their lifetime. But it remains systematic, sufficient, and ever-expanding until it no longer needs to.

When we follow textbooks to learn English — or any language — they try to be "progressive." But the problem is, they're never truly systematic or sufficient. The path we take through textbooks is never a systematic spiral. What we think is "progressive" often turns out to be a crooked line.

![spiral-lineage](/images/spiral-lineage.png)

That's why, despite years of study and practice, we often freeze in real-world situations.

When learning a second language, the main challenge is that we're not constantly in a real-world environment. Plus, as adults, we've missed the natural spiral path. And all available textbooks or courses are never truly systematic or sufficient.

In my growing-up years, there was no way around this. It wasn't until I was over fifty that I learned that when people describe their location, they don't say "describe your **location**," but rather "describe your **bearings**." Before that, I had no idea *bearings* had this meaning in such a common context. For example, determining one's position, *to take one's bearings*; getting familiar with the surroundings, *to get rid of one's bearings*; losing direction, *to lose one's bearings*.

Even something as common as a screwdriver — I only knew it as *a screwdriver*. If I wanted to be more specific, I'd freeze.

> * **tip**: the end of the screwdriver

Plus, there's another awkward truth: the "system" we naturally learn in our native language includes a lot of things we've never consciously organized or summarized. These are crucial for any "system" to function normally.
