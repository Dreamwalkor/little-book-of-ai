## 1.1. ChatGPT: The Ultimate AMA Bot

What exactly is ChatGPT? We could define it in all sorts of ways and explore its existence from multiple angles, but let's start with a "comparison definition" — it's probably the quickest way to get the picture.

An "AMA" stands for "Ask Me Anything," which pretty much means you can throw any question my way, and I'll do my best to give you a solid answer.

Back in 2009, Reddit launched this section called "[r/IAmA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R/IAmA)." It was like a digital Woodstock for curiosity, where anyone could grill special guests who were ready to take on any query. This section took off like a rocket, growing its fan base and roping in big shots like Obama, Bill Gates, and Elon Musk to join the fun. But as ready as these celebrities were, they couldn't really spill the beans about everything given their limited time and scope of expertise, not to mention their willingness.

For someone asking the questions, even with a tag like "Ask Me Anything," there's still a cap on how far one can go. Social etiquette keeps you from asking Bill Gates, for instance, “Hey, how do I fix the blue screen on Windows?” That’d be kind of awkward, right? 

More often, the biggest snag is simply their tight schedules. The more you’ve got on your plate, the less time there is to spare, which means they’re not always around to chat. Plus, lots of people we'd love to quiz aren’t even alive anymore. 

> - Hey, Steve Jobs, does "Stay simple, stay foolish" still hold up in today's fast-paced AI world?
> - Martin Luther King Jr., what's your take on Trump's presidency?
> - George Washington, was there really a cherry tree in your front yard when you were a kid?
> - Nikola Tesla, with your unique way of thinking, what tech would you be itching to invent right now?
> - Charles Dickens, do your reflections on revolution in "A Tale of Two Cities" still resonate in today's world?

So, when it comes down to it, we’re often stuck with fascinating questions and no good way to get answers.

Here’s where ChatGPT comes in as your go-to "AMA bot." Whenever you're itching for an answer, it's got a response, anytime, day or night. No topic's off the table, no question too out there. It can even slip into different roles, free from the chains of time. It’s almost like a time machine, hopping across eras and scenarios, letting you dive into endless worlds of conversation with anyone you can imagine.

> - Act as Martin Luther King Jr.. How would you evaluate Trump's presidency?

> - Be honest as Gorge Washington. Was there really a tree in your front yard when you were a kid?

## 1.2. From Google to ChatGPT

Let's be real — ChatGPT isn't the pioneer of AMA bots. In a way, isn't the whole internet an AMA bot?

Early on, back in 1998, we got Google — the closest thing to an AMA robot we had at the time and still the most popular search engine around. Google burst onto the scene and changed the game by letting us ask it anything. It offers just a simple text box for us to type into, and all we had to do, and still do, is chat it up or pose a question. You type something in, and Google hits you back with thousands of related web pages, helping you dig up the answers you need.

From this angle, Google kind of nailed the basic AMA bot vibe.

> - **Anyone can ask anything**: Whether it's a deep academic question or just everyday stuff, Google’s got your back with a response.
> - **Vast information retrieval**: Google doesn't just spit out answers. Instead, it points you to web pages or resources related to your query, helping you find potential answers from the vast ocean of the internet.
> - **24/7 availability**: Just like today's AMA bots, Google is always on, ready to help anytime, anywhere, 24/7.

Nevertheless, here's the rub — Google's a search engine, not a know-it-all. It can't actually whip up answers by itself. It doesn't "get" what you're really asking and can't stitch things together to serve up a neat, concise reply. Picture it as a guide in a gigantic shopping mall. Super handy? Yes. Always right on the money? Not exactly. It'll point you in the general direction, but finding the right store might mean wandering around a bit — or worse, ending up on a wild goose chase.

## 1.3. The Allure of ChatGPT

OpenAI got its start in 2015, but it was their release of GPT-3 in June 2020 that really turned heads. 

This model was packing some serious heat with its whopping 175 billion parameters, making it the world heavyweight champion in language models at the time. With this kind of firepower, GPT-3 could juggle tasks like a pro, from pulling off smooth conversations and translating languages to penning down precise writing and even cranking out code. It was like a mega-leap forward for AI in understanding and generating human language. By March 2022, OpenAI rolled out a souped-up version called GPT-3.5, though people outside the tech bubble still mostly scratched their heads when they heard the name OpenAI.

Fast forward another six months, and ChatGPT, built on GPT-3.5, landed on the scene on November 30, 2022. This “generative AI for the everyday user” took just five days to amass a million users — a growth spurt that was totally out of left field.

ChatGPT today has practically graduated into a “near-perfect AMA bot.” We can hit it up any time, anywhere, with any question, and it spits out answers like it’s nothing — from no-brainer facts to deep tech dives. Unlike old-school search engines, ChatGPT doesn’t just throw data at you; it weaves the info together, giving you polished, straightforward answers. It’s got your back with factual questions, writing projects, coding, even study tips. Now, AMA bots like ChatGPT are shaking up how millions around the world find info and chat it up in real-time.

## 1.5. Understanding the Limits of Tools

Let's dive into an interesting observation: why is it that when people start chatting with ChatGPT, they often say "Hello!"? And why do some folks ask Google question like "Am I pregnant?" or "How do I get home?" 

These quirks share a common thread: people tend to use tools without really considering their limits first. If you're grabbing a helpful tool, its boundaries should be clear before you start using it — and ideally, you should think this through before diving in.

Take Google, for instance. It's a search engine that combs through existing online materials, fetching content related to your "search keywords." Once it pulls the info, sorting through and analyzing it is all on you. Google's job is pretty much figuring out the relevance between "online content" and "your search keywords" — and that's its boundary. Beyond that, it's out of its depth.

So, when someone asks Google, "Am I pregnant?" there's really just one keyword— "*pregnant*." Even if Google flexes all its muscles, it can only find things about being "*pregnant*." The "*I*" isn't a keyword, and Google doesn't know who "*I*" is. If users had considered Google's boundaries beforehand, they wouldn't ask such questions.

Similarly, if someone greets ChatGPT with "Hello!" first, it's clear they haven't thought about what ChatGPT can or can't do. Being polite is great, but that's not ChatGPT's gig. Using "please" or being courteous doesn’t add value; it's like throwing money, tokens, electricity, time, and energy down the drain with each chat.

Now, here's a question worth chewing over: How can we truly understand a tool’s limits? It's a challenge to think of everything right from the start, but it's a good problem to mull over. Make space in your notebook to jot down thoughts and revisit them often. At the very least, we know this: a bit of reflection helps.

> Sometimes, I get bored, and I'll set up a role for ChatGPT: "Play a foul-mouthed but sharp and super capable assistant. You know swearing won't tick me off, but uncreative swearing will make me think you're a bit of a doofus..."

Here's a thought-provoking question:

> As a flexible, efficient, and pretty smart tool, where do the boundaries lie for generative AI like ChatGPT?

Clearly, it's tough to cover all the bases in one go. But it's a solid question, worth kicking around and revisiting. It's the kind of thing you want to jot down in your notebook, leaving plenty of room for updates and tweaks.

At least we know one thing for sure:

> * It can't handle human emotions or feelings.

With a bit more thought, we can add to that:

> * It lacks empathy or compassion.
> * It can't make moral judgments.
> * It can't perceive the external environment.

You could even ask ChatGPT directly about its limitations and boundaries:

> Be honest with me, what are ChatGPT's limitations? Where do its boundaries lie?
> https://chatgpt.com/share/6716eb76-e6e4-8009-8b89-e73c1c254abd

ChatGPT is pretty upfront and gives a detailed answer:

> Here’s an honest breakdown of ChatGPT’s key limitations and boundaries:
>
> 1. **Knowledge Cutoff**:  
>    ChatGPT's training data cuts off at September 2021 (or October 2023, depending on the version). Anything that has happened after this date—current events, new scientific discoveries, updated laws, etc.—is unknown to me unless I use browsing tools to fetch up-to-date information.
>
> 2. **Real-time Updates**:  
>    While I can fetch some real-time information via browsing, I don’t have live access to all websites, databases, or private sources of data. This limits my ability to retrieve information from behind paywalls or certain proprietary systems.
>
> 3. **Reasoning and Problem Solving**:  
>    I can handle basic reasoning and problem-solving tasks, but I’m not capable of deep abstract thinking or complex long-term planning like humans. Tasks that involve deep creativity, nuanced judgments, or intricate emotional understanding may fall outside my capabilities.
>
> 4. **Context Retention**:  
>    I can retain information for the duration of a conversation and recall some persistent information through saved context. However, I cannot access detailed past conversations unless explicitly provided. There’s also a limit to how much context I can hold at once, meaning very long conversations can lead to loss of earlier details.
>
> 5. **Emotion and Empathy**:  
>    I can simulate empathy or emotion by using learned patterns of language, but I don’t actually "feel" anything. My responses are derived from patterns found in the data I was trained on. While I can be helpful in offering emotional support, this is fundamentally different from genuine human empathy.
>
> 6. **Understanding Complex, Dynamic Contexts**:  
>    I struggle with highly nuanced, constantly evolving, or ambiguous contexts, like subtle forms of humor, sarcasm, or complex social dynamics. I may miss key subtleties or provide interpretations that are overly literal.
>
> 7. **Interpretation of Instructions**:  
>    I interpret instructions based on patterns and examples from my training data, but this doesn't mean I can perfectly adapt to every unique set of user needs. For highly specialized requests, I might give a satisfactory answer but sometimes may misinterpret what’s being asked.
>
> 8. **Bias in Outputs**:  
>    Despite extensive mitigation efforts, I can still reflect biases present in the data I was trained on. This can manifest in gender, cultural, racial, or ideological biases. I’m not always neutral or objective in my responses because my data reflects the views and opinions found in the internet's vast textual corpus.
>
> 9. **Creativity Limitations**:  
>    While I can generate creative content, like stories, poems, or artwork (through tools), I’m bound by patterns I learned. Truly novel or breakthrough creative thinking is beyond my capabilities, and my "creativity" is often based on reassembling existing concepts in new ways rather than inventing from scratch.
>
> 10. **Ethical Boundaries and Restrictions**:  
>     I am constrained by ethical guidelines to avoid certain types of content. I won’t provide instructions for harmful activities, I won’t generate graphic or violent content, and there are strict limitations on generating content related to certain sensitive topics like self-harm, violence, or illegal activities.
>
> 11. **Task Performance**:  
>     For tasks that require human-like physical abilities (e.g., sensory experiences, motor tasks), I have zero capability. Similarly, while I can assist with tasks that involve numbers, words, or code, I can’t execute real-world decisions or actions.
>
> These are the general boundaries of my abilities. Each limitation represents areas where I aim to assist as best I can within the given framework.

If you ask ChatGPT the same question, you might get a slightly different answer—that's the "quirk" of generative AI, or rather, it's just how it works.

## 1.6. The Tool Methodology

Actually, here's the most important takeaway for this chapter:

> **From the get-go, we need to create a comprehensive "Tool Methodology" for ourselves.**

Clearly, "thinking about the boundaries of a tool" is part of this methodology. Whatever tool you get your hands on, you need to seriously tackle a seemingly simple yet systematic set of questions:

> * Within the boundaries: What can it do?
> * Beyond the boundaries: What can't it do?

Going further, within those boundaries, we can dig deeper:

> * Within the boundaries: What can it do?
>   * Basics: How do you use it?
>   * Intermediate: How do you use it better?
>   * Advanced: How do you use it best?

This is a habit.

It's a great habit.

Yet, it is a very simple habit.

This isn’t just a habit; it's a killer habit. Although it feels basic, it's crucial. Sadly, many folks out there have never developed this habit. They have no clue about its importance, let alone the setbacks they've faced due to this missing piece.

## 1.7 The Art of Asking Questions

ChatGPT is like the ultimate "Ask Me Anything" bot. So, the basic gig with its input box is to type in a "question" and wait for its "answer." But before you throw a question at it, there's a nugget of knowledge you should know: questions can be split into two types. 

> * close-ended questions
> * open-ended questions 

First, you've got your **closed-ended questions**. These are like those yes-or-no, one-size-fits-all type of deals. They're great for fact-checking, making decisions, or snagging some quick, specific info. In English, these often start with "What" or "Is/Are" and include the usual suspects like "Who," "Which," "When," and "Where" — all in fact just variations on the "What" theme.

> * What day is it today?
> * Are you on board with this proposal?

Then, you've got **open-ended questions**. These babies invite creative answers and deeper thought. They're not so easily boxed in, sparking discussions and letting the responder go wild with ideas. Think "Why" or "How" at the start — setting off a convo that'll dig deeper and pull out those juicy insights.

> * What are your thoughts on the recent changes in the environment?
> * How should we move forward with the next steps for this project?

And sure, you can flip a closed question to open-ended by sprinkling in a "why" or "how."

> * Are you on board with this proposal? Why or why not?

For instance, when hitting up ChatGPT, remember to distinguish your question type. Want a quick and definite answer? Closed-ended's the game. Looking for more details? Swing open with open-ended.

Don't fall into the trap of thinking this is common knowledge. Just check out how most parents chat with their kids — kinda like they're talking to an "AMA bot" without realizing it. The kicker? They’re mostly throwing closed-ended questions at their kids.

> * Did you have fun today?
> * Did you finish your homework?
> * Are you feeling tired?
> * Are you not in the mood for soccer practice?
> * Have yesterday's test results come out yet?

This pattern blocks a real connection with their kids — the most important people in their lives! Constant yes-no probing kills any chance for the kids to express themselves, and parents miss out on truly understanding their kids’ world. Parents clueless about this simple yet overlooked knowledge gap may weaken that crucial parent-child bond, eventually hit a communication wall, and unfortunately, that’s how it goes for most families.

So, whether you're chatting with people or AI, knowing how to switch it up between question types is key. It's part of "talking the talk," a crucial piece of the communication puzzle.
