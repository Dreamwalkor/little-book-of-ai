Now, let's take a look at another issue: "Language bias."

What is "language bias" in AI? Simply put, AI is most friendly to English. Conversely, AI is relatively less friendly to other languages.

This is somewhat unavoidable because AI's "native language" is English. Why? Again, it's an unavoidable reality because the "native language of scientific knowledge"—due to various historical and practical reasons—is English. Whether people like it or not, that's the situation; all scientific knowledge worldwide is primarily recorded and disseminated in English.

Based on this, the highest proportion of the training corpus for AI is in English, which is an unchangeable fact. In the GPT-3 model's training corpus, 93% is English. According to Science and Technology statistics, existing open-source AI training datasets are similarly composed of more than half in English.

Every "culture" certainly has and should have its own native language, and that's perfectly fine. However, the native language of scientific knowledge is singular: English.

There's no such thing as "special science belonging to a particular culture or country." The former Soviet Union had a scientific opportunist named Trofim Denisovich Lysenko, who promoted "Soviet-style science," even extending to "Soviet-style logic." From 1928 to 1964, for over thirty years, Lysenko's views were supported by Stalin, and political interference in science became mainstream. Lysenko's academic views became the main content of "Michurin biology," claiming it was "socialist," "progressive," "materialist," and "proletarian," while "Mendel-Morgan genetics" was "reactionary," "idealistic," "metaphysical," and "bourgeois." During this period, Soviet scientists who attempted to oppose were politically suppressed, imprisoned, or executed, like Nikolai Vavilov, who died in a labor camp. Lysenkoism set Soviet biological genetics back by at least two generations.

Just like scientific knowledge, there's no such thing as "special AI belonging to a particular culture or country." Regardless of who you are, where you are, or what culture you're in, if it's "intelligence," it must align with "scientific knowledge"—otherwise, it can't be called "intelligence." Ultimately, more English corpus is needed for training, and the AI's native language remains English.

## 3.5. The Importance of Complete Conditions

Let's circle back for a moment. Take a close look at the "role definition prompt" for the translation bot. Now, think about it: if you were to write a "role definition prompt" for a translation bot yourself, what would it look like? How would your version differ from mine?

Chances are, the differences would be:

> * Your version might have only two conditions:
>   * If it's in English, translate it to Chinese.
>   * If it's in Chinese, translate it to English.
> * My version, however, includes three conditions:
>   * If it's in English, translate it to Chinese.
>   * If it's in Chinese, translate it to English.
>   * **If it's mixed, translate into the less dominant language.**

As for the extra line in my version — "Provide the translation directly, without additional comments or explanations." — that's not the main focus. It's just a tweak added after some trial and error.

Of course, in a more likely scenario, your version might not have any *conditions* at all. So, you set up two separate bots: one for English-to-Chinese translation and another for Chinese-to-English translation. While that works, those bots aren't exactly "smart."

The key takeaway here is the importance of *having complete conditions*. It's like building a bridge; you want it to be sturdy and reliable, not just functional. By setting up comprehensive conditions, you ensure your translation bot is not only functional but also adaptable and intelligent.

## 3.6. The Journey of Intelligence Begins with George Boole

Let's take a step back and start from the beginning. Humans have many unique traits compared to other animals, and one of the most significant is our ability to create and use tools. This is why we fundamentally believe that without a systematic "tool methodology," a person might not fully embody what it means to be human.

For hundreds of thousands of years after humans started using the first tool (whatever that was, we can only guess), we relied on "non-intelligent tools." Intelligence, as a concept, had never been part of our tools.

In 1854, a minister named George Boole published a book titled *An Investigation of the Laws of Thought*. This work systematically laid out what he had introduced seven years earlier in *The Mathematical Analysis of Logic*: Boolean Algebra and Boolean Logic.

At the time, Boole, a mathematician and philosopher by training, likely couldn't have imagined that his book would become a cornerstone of what we now know as computer science[^1].

Fast forward to 1936, about 90 years after Boolean Algebra and Logic were introduced. Despite the lack of complete hardware, Alan Turing used Boolean Logic and Algebra to design a computer prototype, now known as the "Turing Machine."

In simple terms, Boolean Logic/Algebra is a mathematical framework based on binary logic (true/false or 1/0). It describes basic logical operations like AND, OR, and NOT. These operations are directly applied in digital circuits, forming the core functions of computers. The Turing Machine, an abstract computational model, uses logical operations to execute any computational task, and these operations can be described using Boolean Algebra.

Boolean Algebra provides the low-level logical operation model for computers, while the Turing Machine defines the higher-level algorithm execution process. However, at this point, the "Turing Machine" wasn't a real machine; it was a "pure theoretical mathematical model" on paper.

Just over three years later, in 1940, Claude Shannon applied Boolean Logic to physical circuits, demonstrating that the Turing Machine model could be realized through electrical switches and circuits. Today, all computations and decisions in computers are made using Boolean logic gates.

The rest, as they say, is history. Computers, networks, search engines, and artificial intelligence gradually emerged.

Suddenly, humans had "intelligent tools" at their disposal. So, what's at the core of "intelligence"? It's the ability to make "judgments," which lead to "decisions," followed by "branches" in processes.

This is also what sets humans apart from other animals. Humans can make complex "judgments," form corresponding "decisions," and then analyze specific situations to apply different solutions. If someone lacks "judgment," they can't make the right "decisions" when it matters, leading to rigid thinking or panic.

It's all interconnected, right? So, if someone has poor "judgment" or lacks it altogether, it's not an exaggeration to say they might not fully embody what it means to be human. This isn't an overstatement because "judgment" is at the heart of "intelligence." Without "judgment," there's no "intelligence"—it's that simple.

The amazing thing is that Boolean Algebra/Logic, one of the foundational elements of today's awe-inspiring artificial intelligence, is actually quite simple. It's a mathematical and logical framework that even many elementary school students can understand and master.

## 3.7. Depth and Tenacity

Let's revisit our translation bot role definition prompt. What's so impressive about it? It's smart because it makes **judgments**, which lead to **branches** (or, **decisions**). Each judgment can be calculated using Boolean Algebra, resulting in a "yes" (true) or "no" (false) outcome, allowing for the next step based on the result.

I wrote a book, *The Truth About Thinking*, a slim little volume that includes a "thinking framework":

> * Definition
> * Classification
> * Comparison
> * Causality

This framework is essentially a simple application of Boolean Algebra and Logic. First, you "define" the problem clearly, then list various scenarios, "classify" them, and proceed to "compare" to draw conclusions. Finally, make choices based on "causality" — "because of this comparison, make that choice."

Our version is more powerful, or "smartest," because:

> * If it's in English, translate it to Chinese.
> * If it's in Chinese, translate it to English.
> * **If it's mixed, translate into the less dominant language.**

These three conditions, especially the crucial third one, cover all possibilities, achieving "complete conditions" with no omissions. This allows it to tackle the specific problem (Chinese-English translation) from all angles. In other words, the "translation bot role definition prompt" is effective because it has "judgment" and "complete judgment conditions."

You'll likely have the chance to code someday, even if you think you won't. Your kids certainly will. When you write even a little code, you'll find that "bugs" almost always arise from "missing some special or important conditions." That's it.

We all live in a world full of "problems," and throughout our lives, nearly every task can be abstracted into one: "solving problems." People often say, "Be good at finding problems," which makes me chuckle. Problems are everywhere; we're constantly surrounded by them, aren't we?

Life is essentially "full of challenges," and all "challenges" are essentially "problems." If you can solve problems, especially those others can't, you're a "capital-P Person." If you can't solve problems, even those others can, or if you avoid them altogether, then what are you? I don't know. Politely speaking, you're "just like everyone else."

In language, there aren't many words related to "problems":

> * Solve
> * Analyze
> * Comprehensive
> * In-depth
> * Tenacity

—That's about it. Any others are just synonyms or variations of these five.

If there's a problem, solve it if you can; endure it if you can't. Discerning whether a problem can be *solved* requires wisdom.

To *solve* problems, you must *analyze* them. Analysis should be *comprehensive* and *in-depth*.

"Comprehensive" means "listing as many scenarios as possible" or, conversely, "not missing any possibilities." If there are three scenarios and you only consider two, you're not comprehensive and may suffer for it. Often, doing this step well makes solving the problem easier because you've considered everything, leaving only the task of setting solutions for each scenario.

How do you achieve "comprehensive"? I have three simple tips, understandable by any elementary school student:

> * Clearly define the problem.
> * Think from multiple angles.
> * Accumulate through repeated practice.

"Clearly defining the problem" is crucial. Too broad, and you might list endless scenarios. For example, solving "intelligent translation between any languages" is daunting. Too narrow, like "English to Chinese," requires little scenario listing, perhaps not even "intelligence."

"Thinking from multiple angles" is simply a habit. Whenever possible, ask yourself, "What if I look at it differently?" Once it becomes a habit, you'll find surprises. Surprises stimulate the brain's "dopamine system," and soon, "thinking from a different perspective" becomes addictive — a rare, healthy, fun addiction with real benefits.

"Accumulating through repeated practice" is simple but rarely done. Most people act passively, not proactively, aiming to "finish quickly and move on." They can't complete even one practice thoroughly, let alone "repeated practice." Without it, they encounter fewer surprises, leading to "missing unexpected scenarios" in analysis or thinking, resulting in partial, incomplete work.

Ultimately, it's all about habits. Whether "clearly defining the problem," "thinking from multiple angles," or "accumulating through repeated practice," these are simple habits. They're not determined by intelligence; rather, these habits might determine intelligence — that's the real logic chain.

Always ask yourself, "Am I missing something?" or "Is there something I haven't thought of?" For important issues, develop the habit of "asking every so often, at least three times." Compared to this simple yet crucial habit, school exam scores are insignificant.

Next, let's explore "in-depth." Essentially, it means:

> Seeing the problem behind the problem.

What does "seeing the problem behind the problem" mean? You've seen examples. You think you're learning "AI usage skills," but here you hear and see "tool methodology" and "effective thinking strategies" emphasized. That's me "seeing the problem behind our problem."

I don't think you can't use AI — its interface is simple enough for anyone. If you struggle with AI, the root is deeper... like never seriously considering "tool methodology" or lacking simple yet practical "thinking strategies" in your framework.

This isn't "advanced skill"; it's "causal analysis." It's not "one-time causal analysis" but "iterative causal analysis" — "digging deeper" is a habit. As for "how to do causal analysis," that's beyond this course. Refer to *The Truth About Thinking* for clear, comprehensive guidance.

Finally, there's "*tenacity*." My simple explanation is:

> Tenacity: Facing problems, patiently turning "unsolvable" into "solvable."

Originally, "language bias" might seem "unsolvable," but with "tenacity," you realize it's "temporarily unsolvable" but "potentially solvable with long-term effort." So, investing time in long-term learning and practice to solve it is true "tenacity."

Turning "temporarily unsolvable" into "immediately solvable" — like creating an "intelligent translation bot" — is a modest but commendable form of "tenacity." After all, you learn a lot along the way.

Another example: "algorithm bias" in our previous table is also an "unsolvable" problem. What is algorithm bias? The algorithm itself may not be biased, but based on it and large data corpora, the result may have implicit biases.

For instance, in English, East Asians are called "yellow-skinned," a hidden "algorithm bias" in English corpora. No one on Earth has yellow skin. You've probably only seen "yellow skin" in movie s— like the villain in *Sin City*, the only color character in a black-and-white film, truly yellow-skinned.

![](/images/sin-city-yellow-basterd.png)

In Western religious culture, white represents purity, black its opposite, and yellow is "impure." So, before Christianity spread in Japan, Western literature described Japanese as having yellow skin. During Christianity's prevalence, their skin wasn't yellow. When Christianity was expelled, their skin turned yellow again.

In Western literature, Chinese skin is also yellow. But there's a harmonious misunderstanding. When Western envoys called Chinese "yellow-skinned," Chinese weren't offended — yellow symbolizes "nobility" in Chinese culture, and only emperors wore yellow.

In 1980, cross-strait exchanges resumed, and Hong Kong singer Zhang Mingmin popularized a song, "My Chinese Heart." Its composer, Taiwanese musician Hou Dejian, wrote "Descendants of the Dragon" in 1978, with lyrics:

> Black hair, black eyes, yellow skin, forever descendants of the dragon...

See, "yellow skin," a "cultural bias," hides in English literature, hard to detect. This bias, hidden in big data, trains AI, so even if the algorithm is fine, the bias persists.

Can this be solved? Originally, it seemed impossible. But with "tenacity," two paths emerge.

First, accumulate knowledge to discern these biases. Second, create more in English (not Chinese), even using AI to correct biases in English.

Neither is simple or direct, but those who patiently pursue them are "tenacious" in my eyes.
