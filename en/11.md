# 11. Mastering the Computer

![](/images/dalle/11.png)

Computers are hands down the most powerful tool in today's world. In just a few decades since their debut, they've become the backbone of our lives and society, driving everything from every angle. Everything you use in your daily life has a computer involved somewhere in the process. Whether it's a product or a service, computers are behind the scenes in almost every step of production and sales. In other words, computers are a must-have for any business today — without them, nothing moves.

## 11.1. Many People Can't Use Computers

For the average Joe, computers are a godsend. They're the most affordable and widespread smart tool out there, practically a staple in both life and work. At the very least, they're flexible. But people have taken that flexibility to a whole new level. In everyday life, schools, and workplaces, folks have come up with endless creative ways to use computers.

Back in the day, desktop computers had CD drives — and some folks would pop them open and use the tray as a coffee cup holder. Talk about thinking outside the box!

![](/images/coffee-cup-holder.png)

Or take using a laptop to weigh down the lid on a cup of instant noodles. Later on, gadgets like Kindles, iPads, and big-screen phones got the same treatment. People have even used desktop vents to warm their hands, mouse pads as placemats, or wedged small items like hairpins or paperclips between the keys on a keyboard. These are just the "hardware hacks." When it comes to software, things get even wilder. You could say it's a real bag of tricks.

Think about it: using word processors by hitting the space bar a million times to align text, or pounding the enter key to jump to the next page. Inserting images and centering them with spaces. In spreadsheets, manually adding up numbers instead of using formulas, or copy-pasting instead of dragging to apply the same formula across cells. In browsers, opening new tabs without ever closing them, trying to open local files, or clicking on ads thinking they're part of the content. As for operating systems, dumping everything on the desktop, using file names as version control, and only knowing how to copy-paste with the right-click menu, never touching a shortcut key.

You've probably seen desktops that look like this a million times:

![](/images/desktop-as-only-folder.png)

Over the past 20 or 30 years, it's not like schools or parents haven't cared about "Computer Literacy Education." They have, but the results have been pretty underwhelming, almost zero.

The pace of computer development is just too fast, way beyond what the "let's not change for 200 years" school system can handle. In the late '90s, almost every school in urban China invested in "computer labs." But within a few years, the equipment was outdated. Constantly upgrading gear isn't realistic for schools.

Schools are stuck in the past, and even teachers who weren't originally like that get dragged down, struggling to keep up. Plus, teachers who teach "computer basics" are low on the totem pole — it's not part of the entrance exams, so parents don't care to know them. Before long, their main job becomes maintaining and fixing those aging computers.

This isn't just a local issue; it's global. Over the past few decades, countries worldwide have experienced "rapid development." Initially, workplaces valued employee education, but suddenly, there was no time for it. Everyone was in a rush, anxious, and in a fast-paced economy, employee education became a "nice-to-have." As for "computer literacy"? It was assumed to be something learned before graduation. But in reality?

The environment is pretty rough. Once computers became "essential," shady companies popped up, creating and spreading viruses disguised as antivirus software, hijacking browsers, bundling malware with downloads or pirated software. In such a hostile environment, those who can't use computers become even more clueless — their time, energy, and computing resources get siphoned away.

As time goes on, it's frustrating that the problem hasn't improved. Millennials use computers in more creative, bizarre ways. Most of them have super agile thumbs and instinctively try to swipe screens to turn pages. Almost everyone thinks shutting down a computer is like turning off a phone, and they do it by "long-pressing the power button."

Some even avoid using computers altogether. Who wants to use something they can't figure out? In 2019, a Japanese survey showed that 83.3% of young people around 20 owned smartphones, but only 17.1% had desktop computers. By 2022, according to the PEW Research Center, nearly all teenagers had smartphones, with the rate jumping from 73% in 2014-2015 to 95%. Meanwhile, the percentage of teens with desktop computers dropped, and their computer usage plummeted.

## 11.2. The Cost of Not Using Computers

Computers are — or at least should be — the most crucial tool for the average person. Knowing how to use them is as essential as English or logic in our digital age. It's a core skill, not just some "middleware" know-how.

There are tons of reasons why, but it boils down to one key point:

> Mastering computers boosts your efficiency — whether you're learning, working, or just living your life.

Despite this clear and compelling reason, for most folks, computers are the most underutilized tool at home. If your computer use is mostly about:

> * Basic office software (word processing, email, simple spreadsheets)
> * Browsing the web and social media
> * Gaming and entertainment (watching videos, listening to music)

Then you're in the same boat as over 90% of the planet, tapping into less than 10% of your computer's potential. So, if you spent \$1,500 on a computer, you might as well have thrown away \$1,350. But since this tool impacts your efficiency across the board — in learning, work, and life — the long-term loss is massive, even if it's hard to put a number on it.

Stats show that differences in computer literacy can lead to significant income disparities. For instance, a data analyst who knows advanced data processing techniques (like pivot tables, VBA macros, or Python) might earn 30%-50% more than a regular employee.

If someone earns \$20 an hour and loses just one hour a day due to inefficient computer use (and let's be real, it's often more), that's a yearly loss of $20 \times 365 = \$7,300$. Over a 40-year career, that's a whopping \$292,000. And if you miss out on a higher-paying job because of lacking computer skills, say \$2,000 more per year, that's another \$40,000 over 20 years.

This doesn't even factor in the indirect losses from stunted innovation, career stagnation, or delays in learning new tools and tech. So, conservatively, a person's lifetime economic loss from inefficient computer use could be in the millions. And that's just starting with a $20 hourly wage.

According to a McKinsey Global Institute study, by 2030, tech and automation will shift millions of jobs worldwide, demanding new digital skills. If you don't up your computer game, you'll miss out on high-paying gigs and your earning potential will take a hit.

I can't wrap my head around parents who push their kids into all sorts of extracurriculars but ignore the big stuff. Computer literacy is crucial, yet you rarely see booming training centers for it. Why? Because parents think it's unnecessary since it's not on the entrance exams.

The reason "computer literacy" education hasn't taken off is because people generally "refuse to learn." If they're resistant to learning, forget about "continuous learning." But that's exactly what computer literacy requires — ongoing learning. It's a fast-evolving field, always on the move.

In reality, all major waste in life — whether for individuals, businesses, or society — stems from "not learning." It's so widespread because most people are "program executors," "passive producers," not "active producers" or "creators." Even if they do learn, it's just to "pass the test." With low motivation to learn, or none at all, how can we talk about "continuous learning"?

"Computer literacy" is a basic skill everyone should and can master. For all basic skills, the mantra is "systematic" and "practice like crazy."

Ideally, keep learning and practicing until it becomes a "terminal skill." Computers, like AI, are "universal tools" — useful everywhere, beneficial everywhere.

## 11.3. Computers Under Monopoly

Let's dive into a trend that's flying under the radar for most folks. First, check this out:

> The number of professional computers (servers) on Earth has long surpassed the number of personal computers, and this gap is growing exponentially.

In other words, this super tool — the computer — is increasingly being used professionally, which makes sense. But flip the script, and you'll see:

> This super tool is being monopolized at an accelerating pace.

You might think, "So what?" But here's the kicker — regular people are losing the ability and opportunity to use this super tool at an alarming rate. Forget for a moment whether smartphones are just "distraction devices." Look at it from the angle of "the best tools being monopolized," and you'll see that smartphones and all their apps are just "terminals," things you use the way others dictate.

![](/images/digital-detox.png)

Historically, when something gets monopolized — like production resources or educational access — it sparks widespread resistance or at least heated debate. But this time, it's different. This monopoly is sneaky, almost like water seeping silently into the ground. No one seems to notice that some people or organizations are quietly gaining a relative advantage. Most folks are either blissfully unaware or just don't care, even feeling "thrilled" about it.

Some have raised concerns, though from slightly different angles. Remember the media buzz about "delivery drivers or cabbies trapped in algorithms"? At its core, that's just another outcome of the "monopolization of computer use." In the last week of April 2023, [The New York Times ran a cartoon](https://www.newyorker.com/cartoons/issue-cartoons/cartoons-from-the-april-24-and-may-1-2023-issue):

## 11.4. Democratized Production Tools

People love the perks of tech advancements but rarely think about the freedom it brings.

Back in the day, some crucial tools were off-limits. In the age of cold weapons, not everyone could own swords or armor. During the knight era, sure, you could have cattle or ride a donkey, but horses? Nope, not for everyone. In the Renaissance, firearms were a no-go for common folks.

In ancient times, monopolizing tools was all about power. As time marched on, it became about capital. Take ships, for instance. They've been around forever, but from the 16th to 18th centuries, big ships were out of reach for the average Joe. Fast forward to the Industrial Revolution, and the steam engine was a game-changer, but no ordinary person could own one. Today, powerful tools like massive server clusters or quantum computers are way beyond what regular folks can have.

When I use an iPhone, I often think of it as a "democratized commodity." It's a powerful, effective tool that everyone can own and use — wealth or power just doesn't help. No matter how rich or influential you are, you're using the same device as everyone else. Want more storage? Sorry, can't do. A bigger screen? Nope. You can slap on a diamond-studded case if you want, but it won't boost performance.

The iPhone isn't the first widely used "democratized commodity." Long before it, computers were a rare "democratized tool" in human history. From the get-go, they were "smart" tools with recursive properties. You could use a computer to write programs, even design other computers.

All production boils down to three elements:

> * Means of production
> * Tools of production
> * Methods of production

Words are "democratized means of production," and computers are "democratized tools of production." As for "methods of production," they've always been democratized because "thought" and "thinking" aren't shackled by power or capital. They can even be free from external influences. So, giving up "democratized means" or "democratized tools" is like giving up on "democratized production" and even "democratized creation."

Any error message thrown into Google would yield answers, not just because Google is powerful, but because someone else has likely faced the same issue and someone has probably answered it.

With ChatGPT, it's now down to "just a bit of reading skill." ChatGPT "knows" those questions and answers and can weave them together in a way that's easy for humans to understand. ## 11.8. Revisiting "Reading Comprehension"

The starting point for "basic computer literacy" isn't really about computers at all. It's about a skill many think they've mastered but often haven't — **reading comprehension**.

Every time you hit a snag on your computer, whether it's software or the terminal, you'll get a (usually detailed) "error message." But for many, the sight of all that text flips the "reading switch" to off, leaving them with just one question: "What the heck is going on?!" Panic sets in, or they just give up.

In more than half the cases, if you just take a deep breath and read through the error message, you'll find the solution. Programmers have worked hard to make sure users understand what's happening. Even if something's unclear, you can search for keywords — all it takes is "a bit of reading skill" and "a bit of patience," meaning a little willingness to "search again" and "read another article."

Yet, "reading patience" is a habit most people never developed. School often turns reading into a chore, leaving many to resist or even reject it. As a result, many tools become useless from the get-go. In the "universal tool methodology," only "safety first" comes before "reading the manual." So, for those who struggle with or refuse to read, any tool is essentially useless.

Throughout this book, I've lost count of how many times I've reminded you:

> Just by... you've already outpaced 99% of people.

Let's use this template again:

> When you get any tool, if you take the time to read the manual first, you've already outpaced 99% of people.

Most computer and software "error messages" are in English. In the past, English was a real roadblock for many. But ChatGPT has bulldozed that barrier. Toss any error message its way, and it'll guide you from problem to solution. If it replies in English, no worries — just ask it to "translate the response into Chinese" or any language, and you're off on another direct flight.

It's almost comical. This crucial "basic computer literacy" used to require a bit of "reading skill" and "patience." Now, it's just "reading skill." So let's dig deeper: why is reading comprehension so lacking? After all those years of school, books, and tests, how can this be? What's the root cause?

The root of "poor reading comprehension" is "lack of patience." People want things fast, easy, and direct. The phrase they often use, regardless of gender, age, or nationality, is: "I don't want to hear/read/know all that, just tell me... how to do it!" If someone says this often — not just once in a while — it signals a lack of patience, leading to poor reading comprehension and, ultimately, a lack of knowledge.

So, at the end of the day, it all boils down to "patience." You need patience. And you need enough of it because reading and learning aren't one-time actions. They're recursive processes, requiring patience at every step, often more than before.

In the grand scheme, you can afford to be impatient with many things, but not with text. Impatience with text is the most costly — no need for further proof, right?

Another key reminder: stick to "reading comprehension" as much as possible. "Video tutorials" should be a last resort. Trust me, there are very few things you can only learn through video. Sure, more people prefer videos over text, but haven't you already grasped the importance of text from every angle? In the end, videos and audio are more about entertainment.

## 11.9. Navigating the Maze

When it comes to learning complex knowledge, it's all about building a network in your brain. Writing and reading are challenging because of this. Steven Pinker nailed it when he said:

> "The difficulty of writing lies in the fact that we are trying to turn a network of thoughts into a linear string of words, using a tree structure of syntax."

I see it similarly:

> "The difficulty of reading lies in the fact that constructing a network of thoughts from linear string of wordst, using a tree structure of syntax."

Mastering complex knowledge is like diving into a network with tons of nodes. Each piece of knowledge is a node. The more nodes, the more work. And since nodes are interconnected, the workload grows even more.

In "Graph Theory," a network is made up of "nodes" and "edges." An "edge" is basically a line connecting two nodes.

For a "Directed Complete Graph" (where every node is connected to every other node), with $n$ nodes, there are $ E = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$ edges. So, 1 node has no edges; 2 nodes have 1 edge; 3 nodes have 3 edges; 4 nodes have 6 edges... It's like a maze. For someone exploring it, traversing all nodes and edges is a massive task.

The kicker? Computer-related knowledge is a "network with ever-increasing nodes" — meaning the workload keeps growing. And not only are the nodes increasing, but they're doing so at an accelerating pace — so the workload ramps up even more.

But here's the thing: while the workload is huge and growing, the difficulty isn't. Reading isn't hard — unless you're impatient. The real challenge is having endless patience to tackle the increasing workload.

Of course, networks with more knowledge points are usually more valuable. This ties into [**Metcalfe's Law**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metcalfe%27s_law), which is also rooted in "Graph Theory." The "extension" is just asking the same question in different contexts — "Where else does this apply?":

> **The value of a network is proportional to the square of the number of its nodes.**

As for the secret to "navigating the maze," it's simple: **patiently read through it** and, when necessary, **reread it**... That's all there is to it.
